Problem Statement
Imagine waking up one morning to find the quiet horizon of your neighborhood replaced by the
steel skeleton of a massive industrial complex. Or perhaps there is no visible change, yet the
looming impact remains. The electricity bill has suddenly spiked. The water from the kitchen
faucet has become unreliable. No one knocked on your door; there was no notice, and no one
asked for your consent. In the community there now exists an invisible giant—a data center. It
will impact your daily life while remaining behind windowless walls and nondisclosure
agreements.
With the global excitement surrounding artificial intelligence (AI), physical facilities—data
centers—have been popping up in order to meet this increasing demand. Illinois, as it turns out,
is one such frontier for this digital infrastructure. But the construction is so fast—so
“hasty”—that in the pursuit of the quickest, cheapest, solutions, the very communities that host
these facilities are being taken advantage of.
While the rest of the world profits from the speed and convenience of AI, residents are left to
shoulder the weight of their local giant’s physical footprint. Technological progress is only as
valuable as the stability it provides to the people hosting it, requiring a balance between
industrial speed and civic responsibility. We must strive for a future where technological
progress does not come at the expense of community agency, and environmental health. We must
prioritize a model of mutual success wherein corporate growth and community wellbeing rise
together.


Introduction
The "annoyance threshold" is a term that seeks to define the "tipping point" of a person or
group's tolerance towards a particularly bothersome stressor. It can be applied to many different
contexts: from a legal requirement to a commonly accepted social norm. To contextualize it
better through example, think of how one is allowed to make ever so much of a ruckus in public
before their behavior is deemed socially “unacceptable” or “annoying” by those around them.
For a more formal example, think of how a factory is allowed to release ever so much pollutant
before they breach a pre-defined legal “limit”. In all cases, we see this idea of being allowed to
do or emit something bothersome only to a certain, agreed upon or inherently understood, extent.
Though the annoyance threshold sounds like an abstract concept at first, it is clear that it silently
appears in our lives on a regular basis. As we will all be there at one point or another, we uphold
its existence in hopes of making the affected group less “annoyed”. In the context of AI data
centers, unlike most other industrial infrastructure, this “tipping point” has yet to be legally
defined at the federal level. This means that in the meantime, it must be defined on a
state-by-state basis. We have the power to advocate for it at the state level. In order to do so, we
must first define it. In working towards this definition of the “annoyance threshold”, for this
project we have chosen to summarize the issue of AI infrastructure into three critical points:

    2.​ Social: Lack of Transparency and Community Devaluation

With the above in mind, we posit that this concept of the "annoyance threshold" must be applied
to the relationship between these physical facilities and their surrounding communities.
Decisions are being made in corporate boardrooms and closed-door zoning meetings, leaving
residents to deal with the fallout. It is not fair for local residents to unknowingly shoulder the
physical and financial burdens of a global AI race that they themselves never joined.
This project, “The People v. Hasty AI,” seeks to close this gap by mapping these facilities to the
real-world stressors of the people living near them.


What exactly are we trying to accomplish?
We want to advocate for more transparency; to bring awareness to impacted communities. We
believe that by making the "invisible giant" visible, we can shift the power balance back to the
people.
As such, this paper will serve as a comprehensive assessment of the true cost of Illinois’s data
center boom. We will begin by laying the groundwork: examining existing research on the
stressors inherent to these facilities. This will be followed by a deep-dive of case
studies—including the successful community opposition in Naperville and the high-profile
conflict of xAI in Memphis—to illustrate the real-world stakes. We will then present a “Call to
Action”, of sorts, for specific areas in Illinois (DeKalb, Elk Grove Village, and Chicago) wherein
we will revisit and flesh out the “annoyance threshold” and provide resources for residents to
contact their respective representatives. We will emphasize the importance of the new Illinois
Senate bill, SB 4016—”The POWER Act”— and encourage those interested to look into it
further.
To ground this research, we will introduce an interactive data visualization tool that maps these
facilities against environmental statistics and socioeconomic indicators.
Finally, we will argue that regulation, rather than being an obstacle to progress, can serve as a
catalyst for innovation. We will present potential compromises that serve the interests of both the
company and the community, ensuring that technological growth no longer requires a sacrifice of
local peace and stability.

Background

Recent scholarship increasingly recognizes that AI infrastructure is not immaterial. It is deeply
tied to physical systems from electricity grids and water reserves to regional environmental
conditions. Researchers studying data center expansion emphasize that AI workloads are rapidly
becoming a major driver of global electricity demand; on top of this, it is also introducing
significant water consumption through cooling processes that are rarely visible to the public.
Estimates suggest that AI-related data center activity could generate tens of millions of tons of
carbon emissions annually while consuming hundreds of billions of liters of water worldwide,
highlighting the growing environmental footprint associated with large-scale computation (de
Vries-Gao; Patel et al.). These findings challenge the popular perception of AI as a purely digital
technology. We should instead position it as an industrial infrastructure with measurable
ecological consequences.

Beyond energy use, researchers have begun examining how these environmental impacts
translate into lived community experiences. Lifecycle analyses show that data centers contribute
indirectly to air pollution through electricity generation, producing measurable public health
burdens that may disproportionately affect nearby populations (Han et al.). Social science
research further argues that community concerns often stem not only from environmental
exposure but from procedural issues such as limited transparency, restricted access to project
information, and insufficient local participation in decision-making processes (Sovacool, Upham,
and Monyei). Policy analyses similarly note that rapid data center expansion in the United States
frequently occurs faster than governance frameworks can adapt. This creates uncertainty about
accountability, disclosure, and long-term community protections (Walker and Goldsmith).

Further exacerbating the issue, other sources connect these developments to broader questions of
environmental and data justice. Researchers argue that technological infrastructures distribute
benefits and harms unevenly, especially when environmental monitoring data or operational
disclosures remain inaccessible to affected communities (Vera et al.; Lucivero). At the same
time, emerging research on energy burden demonstrates that increases in large-scale electricity
demand can intersect with socioeconomic vulnerability, potentially shifting infrastructure costs
onto residents through higher energy expenditures (Garland et al.). Together, these studies
establish a foundation for examining AI data centers not only as technological innovations, but
as socio-environmental systems whose impacts must be evaluated through environmental
equality, governance transparency, and economic fairness.

Case Studies

From Global Expansion to Local Reality

AI infrastructure is often discussed at a global scale, framed through innovation, computational
growth, and economic competitiveness. Yet data centers are ultimately local projects. They
occupy land, draw electricity and water, alter traffic and construction patterns, and introduce new
industrial rhythms into surrounding communities. Across the United States, recent development
trends show that while AI systems appear digital, their impacts are material and geographically
uneven. Communities experience these projects not as abstract technological progress but
through everyday environmental conditions, planning decisions, and economic tradeoffs.

To maintain consistency, each of the following cases will be examined through the
aforementioned three interconnected dimensions: environmental, social, and economic. The
environmental dimension focuses on physical and operational impacts such as power
infrastructure expansion, generator emissions, land conversion, lighting, and noise. The social
dimension considers transparency, participation, and community consent during planning and
approval processes. The economic dimension evaluates how projects are justified through
investment and redevelopment narratives while also recognizing potential long-term costs or

A Glimpse at an Established Precedent: The Case of South Memphis and xAI

The city of Memphis provides a glimpse at the impact of unregulated AI infrastructure. Dubbed
xAI’s “Colossus,” this project encapsulates the idea of “hasty” AI expansion, bypassing
traditional multi-year planning cycles. This speed was achieved at a significant cost to the local
community of Boxtown in South Memphis. Worse, rather than simply existing as an, admittedly
unfortunate, addition to the residential background, community members would report tangible
impacts on their daily lives.

Before examining what’s at stake in Illinois, we will first assess what has already happened in
Memphis, following the same environmental, social, and economic cascade of impacts.

Using data from public NASA satellites as well as the European Space Agency, researchers at

Because these turbines were labeled as "temporary," they bypassed the Environmental Protection
Agency’s (EPA) oversight required for permanent stationary plants.

But what exactly is meant by “temporary”? And how long does this “temporary” last? In
environmental law, a "temporary" source—often labeled as "temporary-mobile"—is one that
remains on a site for less than one consecutive year. It’s typically installed by mounting turbines
onto flatbed trailers or shuffling units until the permitted time elapses. Companies have
historically evaded the stringent pre-construction permits required for stationary power plants via
this “temporary” loophole (Environmental Protection Agency; Ireland).

To suggest that those unhappy with the smog should “just leave” is not a solution; it is a
normalization of corporate trespassing that treats local well-being as a secondary concern. No
company should have the unilateral right to degrade a living situation without warrant. No
person should be forced to make the choice between their homes and health, especially when the
cause is a corporate third party that moved in without notice. Further, some residents simply do
not have the financial mobility to uproot their lives, making them captive to whatever pollutants
the "invisible giant" breathes out. Within this framing, the choice of location for Colossus tells a
more intentional story. Boxtown is a predominantly Black community with a median income of
approximately $37,000—less resources to “fight back”. Critics argue it is no coincidence that the
"biggest supercomputer in the world" was placed in a neighborhood with low political resistance
and high economic vulnerability (Chow). Accepting this precedent in Memphis normalizes a
concept that should be unthinkable, putting every community—including those in Illinois—at
risk of becoming the next site.

The social dimension of the Memphis case is defined by a complete lack of transparency. The
project was negotiated in secret, leaving the community completely in the dark until the facility
was operational. This "ask-for-permission-later" approach is deeply disrespectful to a community
that has already, historically, suffered from environmental hazards. The NAACP and Southern
Environmental Law Center have since sent separate intent-to-sue notices for violations of the
Clean Air Act, but the damage to the social fabric is already done (Chow; More Perfect Union;
Hilt). Residents feel that their agency was traded for corporate speed, setting a dangerous
precedent where tech companies can bypass local democratic processes as long as they promise
"innovation”.

While the facility brings investment to the region, it also introduces massive ratepayer risk. If the
grid requires billions in upgrades to support xAI’s expansion, those costs could be shifted onto
the very families who are already struggling with the environmental fallout. Despite the outcry,
the cycle of development continues: plans are already underway for an even larger data center in
the nearby neighborhood of Whitehaven.

Stepping back briefly, it is important to recognize the wider issue of industrial plants operating
near residential areas. Abuse of residential zoning in Memphis predates xAI’s Colossus, and
thus, the entire problem of industrial encroachment cannot be attributed to a single company.
However, through the global excitement surrounding AI, we are seeing a modern revival of this
trend toward hasty infrastructure. While we should ideally seek to reform the operations of all
industries operating in residential areas, this project narrows its focus to the specific expansion of
AI infrastructure. The "hasty" model observed here is a repeatable strategy that can—and will, if
left unchecked—be applied to other vulnerable areas.

If Memphis represents this “hasty” model at its most extreme, Illinois represents opportunity.
Unlike South Memphis, where the industrial footprint is already deeply entrenched, the

In the Context of Illinois

Illinois provides a particularly useful setting for examining these dynamics because it contains
examples across the full lifecycle of data center development. Within a single state, operational
facilities coexist with newly proposed campuses and at least one high-profile rejected project.
This range allows the analysis to move beyond hypothetical impacts and instead observe how
expectations, negotiations, and outcomes differ depending on location and community context.

The following case studies therefore shift the discussion from general debates about AI
infrastructure toward grounded observations drawn from Illinois communities themselves.

Proposed and Under-Development Projects: Negotiation and Uncertainty

Proposed projects reveal impacts more clearly because communities encounter them before
normalization occurs. The Edged “Project Vector” proposal near the existing Meta campus in
DeKalb demonstrates how environmental concerns emerge during planning stages. Public
hearings and local reporting highlight discussions surrounding lighting mitigation, stormwater
management, and infrastructure integration, indicating attempts to anticipate nuisance impacts
before construction begins (City of DeKalb, “Edged Development Approved”).

In Minooka, planning materials and community outreach events surrounding a proposed Equinix
data center show how environmental and economic narratives intersect. Village documents
emphasize investment potential while residents raise questions about farmland conversion and
long-term water use (Village of Minooka). Rural proposals such as the CyrusOne campus in
Sangamon County further illustrate how large projected electrical loads and utility planning
become central issues when projects enter agricultural landscapes, where industrial infrastructure
represents a visible shift in land identity (Sangamon County).

Socially, these projects generate structured participation through hearings, open houses, and
public comment periods, creating clearer records of negotiation between developers and
residents. Economically, redevelopment proposals in Hoffman Estates and Mount Prospect are
often framed as adaptive reuse of former corporate campuses, presenting data centers as
successors to earlier economic eras rather than entirely new industrial activity (“Compass Kicks
off Development of Its First Campus in Illinois”; “ComEd and CLoudHQ Break Ground on New
Hyperscale Data Center in Mount Prospect”). These cases highlight uncertainty, where benefits
remain projected while impacts remain speculative.




                                                                                               16
Denied Development in Illinois: Community Limits and the Annoyance Threshold

The rejected Karis Critical proposal in Naperville provides an important counterpoint to both
operational and proposed projects. Located on the former Lucent campus, the proposal
underwent months of hearings and revisions before the city council ultimately denied the
conditional use permit in January 2026. News coverage consistently identifies resident concerns
related to diesel backup generators, air quality, noise, and proximity to homes as central factors
influencing the decision (Cox; NCTV17 Staff; ABC7 Chicago Staff).

Environmentally, the debate focused on routine operational impacts rather than catastrophic risk,
demonstrating how cumulative everyday stressors can shape perception. Socially, the extended
review process and high public turnout reflected sustained community engagement and
skepticism toward mitigation assurances. Economically, supporters emphasized redevelopment
and investment potential, while opponents questioned whether benefits justified long-term
neighborhood impacts.

As a case study, Naperville illustrates that acceptance of AI infrastructure is not inevitable. When
environmental concerns align with strong social mobilization and uncertain economic tradeoffs,
communities may determine that a project exceeds their acceptable threshold. The denial
therefore functions not as an anomaly but as evidence that local governance remains a decisive
factor in shaping the trajectory of AI development.6



Call to Action

Annoyance Threshold

It is here that we must revisit this concept of the “annoyance threshold”.7 As previously
established, this threshold is the silent boundary of our social and legal lives—the point where a
"ruckus in public" becomes an arrestable offense, or a factory’s exhaust becomes a legal
violation. It is the "tipping point" where a bothersome activity crosses the line from a nuisance to
a breach of the community's well-being. While the term may sound subjective, it is a
well-documented psychological phenomenon. As far back as 1956, researcher Walter Spieth
demonstrated that an individual's tolerance for noise—and (arguably) by extension, any
persistent environmental stressor—is not fixed. It is a product of their environment.
In his study, “Annoyance threshold judgments of bands of noise”, Spieth asked participants to set
a noise level that would "annoy them if it were present most of the time." The results were stark:

In the context of the Illinois AI boom, this study validates a critical point: a community’s
"accepted limit" for industrial stress is being artificially inflated. By the time an "invisible giant"
breaks ground in a quiet community, the industry has already decided that the residents will
simply learn to be bothered less. We must stop placing the burden on the resident to "adjust" to
the thrum of cooling fans, the spike in utility bills, or the strain on local water. Instead, the
burden must be placed on the industry to quiet down. We argue that legislation must recognize
these thresholds before construction begins, and continue to enforce them throughout the
facility’s lifespan. We must protect the "right to be annoyed" as a defense of a stable, quiet, and
affordable community. To be annoyed is to recognize that a boundary has been crossed; we
cannot let "hasty" infrastructure move that boundary for us.

    3.​ Clean Energy Requirements: Incentivizes "Bring Your Own Power" models to ensure the

Platform Design and Analytical Methodology

Live platform: The People v. AI
GitHub repository: https://github.com/Laasya-73/SoReMo-S26-people-v-hasty-ai.git



Purpose
This section documents the ins-and-outs of our data visualization tool; how it evolved from a
static map into a multi-workspace analytical system for evaluating AI/data center expansion in
Illinois. The platform is designed to answer the policy question:
Where are data centers concentrated, what burdens already exist in those counties, and how
much additional pressure is introduced under alternative growth assumptions?



Analytical Framing
The platform is built around two complementary frameworks:
     Layered Narrative Framework​
     A guided sequence helps users move from location to consequence:
          ●​ where data centers are now/planned,
          ●​ current county stress conditions,
          ●​ populations likely to be exposed,
          ●​ projected burden under growth scenarios.

     Annoyance Threshold Framework​
      A composite burden concept operationalizes “community strain” using cumulative
     indicators, including:
           ●​ air quality stress (e.g., AQI-related signal),
           ●​ water stress,
           ●​ household energy burden,
           ●​ social vulnerability-oriented indicators.
     Counties above a user-defined threshold are flagged for closer review.




                                                                                                 21
Data and Layer Architecture
The system integrates curated project records and external datasets into a county-level decision
context. Key layer families include:
   ●​ Infrastructure footprint layers: Existing, Proposed, Denied, Registry (all known Illinois
      sites), County outlines.
   ●​ Environmental layers: air-quality indicators, water-stress indicator(s), and related stress
      context.
   ●​ Community/equity layers: demographic and vulnerability-oriented county indicators.
   ●​ Energy context layers: county energy-burden and electricity-use context.
   ●​ Registry expansion layer: global/US infrastructure records for comparative context, with
      Illinois filtering.
This architecture allows users to compare facility presence with pre-existing county conditions in
the same interface.



Core Quantitative Logic

Pressure Model
Pressure is modeled as a weighted index so assumptions are explicit and auditable.
Current Pressure

       Current Pressure = (Existing wE)

Planned Pressure

       Planned Pressure = (Existing wE) + (Proposed wP) + (Denied wD)

Pressure Delta (Change in Pressure)

       ΔPressure = Planned Pressure - Current Pressure

Default weights are exposed to users and can be adjusted in the Scenario Studio (recommended
baseline, alternative profiles, and custom setup). Registry points are reference coverage and are
excluded from pressure scoring.



Annoyance Threshold Score
We attempt to define the annoyance threshold arithmetically. It combines multiple burden
dimensions into one interpretable screening signal. In implementation terms, this is an


                                                                                                    22
equal-weight composite of normalized county stress indicators (air, water, energy, and social
vulnerability context), with threshold-based county flagging for decision attention.

Limitations and Next Steps
Current outputs are screening and prioritization tools, not causal attribution models. Limitations
include source heterogeneity, uneven metadata completeness, and county-level aggregation
constraints for some variables.
Future work should strengthen:
   ●​ time-series trend integration,
   ●​ utility/grid and emissions granularity,
   ●​ additional validated source harmonization,
   ●​ uncertainty communication for composite thresholds.



Discussion and Policy Implications

Interpreting the Platform Outputs
The platform’s results should be read as decision-support evidence, not as a deterministic ranking
of “good” versus “bad” counties. Its primary value is comparative: it shows where infrastructure
concentration, environmental stress, and community vulnerability overlap, and how those
overlaps change under alternative growth assumptions.
In this sense, the outputs are most useful for identifying where additional scrutiny is warranted,
rather than replacing formal permitting, environmental review, or public consultation.



Why This Matters for Illinois Data Center Governance
Illinois is already experiencing uneven data center concentration, especially in and around major
logistics and grid-access corridors. The project demonstrates that infrastructure siting discussions
can be improved when they move from isolated project review to cumulative county context.
By combining footprint layers with stress indicators, the platform helps decision-makers ask a
stronger policy question:


                                                                                                     26
Is a county being asked to absorb incremental AI infrastructure on top of already elevated burden
conditions?



Policy Use Cases
The system supports at least four practical governance uses:
   ●​ Pre-screening for permitting bodies: Counties with high planned pressure and high
      baseline stress can be flagged for enhanced review criteria, mitigation conditions, or
      phased approvals.
   ●​ Hearing preparation and community review: County Intelligence Briefing outputs
      translate technical signals into concise narratives that stakeholders could potentially
      discuss in public meetings.
   ●​ Scenario negotiation: Impact Scenario Studio allows agencies, developers, and
      community groups to test alternative assumptions and compare outcomes transparently.
   ●​ Evidence traceability: Registry and source-quality panels improve trust by showing
      where records come from and how confidence is assigned.



Equity and Environmental Justice Implications
A central contribution of this project is making equity analysis operational rather than rhetorical.
By co-locating demographic/vulnerability indicators with infrastructure and stress signals, the
platform helps reveal where expansion may reinforce existing disparities.
This does not prove disproportionate impact by itself; however, it identifies counties where
equity-sensitive governance safeguards are likely necessary before additional approvals.



Methodological Implications
The project illustrates a useful middle ground between static reporting and fully automated
forecasting:
   ●​ static maps are often descriptive but not actionable;
   ●​ black-box models can be predictive but hard to contest publicly;
   ●​ this platform uses explicit assumptions, editable weights, and interpretable outputs.
That design choice is important for policy environments, where legitimacy depends on clarity,
contestability, and public accountability.




                                                                                                  27
Limits of Interpretation
Three limits should be stated clearly:
     Composite scores simplify reality​
      Pressure and threshold metrics are abstractions; they support comparison but do not
     capture all local conditions.
     Data completeness varies by source​
      Some indicators are richer than others, and registry coverage differs across providers and
     regions.
     County scale can mask sub-county variation​
      Intra-county inequalities and neighborhood-level exposures may not be fully visible in
     county-level summaries.
These limits do not reduce usefulness, but they define appropriate use: screening, prioritization,
and policy framing.



Recommended Policy Direction
Based on this project’s outputs and workflow, a practical policy path is to adopt:
   ●​ tiered review triggers for high-pressure/high-burden counties,
   ●​ phased approvals linked to measurable checkpoints,
   ●​ public disclosure requirements for siting assumptions and cumulative burden context,
   ●​ county-specific mitigation commitments when threshold indicators are elevated.
This aligns infrastructure growth with procedural fairness and environmental accountability.



Broader Significance
Although developed for Illinois, the framework is transferable. Any region facing
AI-infrastructure growth can adapt the same structure: footprint mapping, stress overlays,
scenario testing, and explainable briefing outputs.
The broader research contribution is therefore not only a map, but a replicable governance
method for evaluating AI infrastructure expansion under uncertainty.


Recommendations
To move from "hasty" expansion to sustainable integration, we will now propose the following
supplemental solutions designed to build upon the foundation of SB 4016, specifically tuned for
data centers built around residential areas.


                                                                                                   28
The cheapest solution is not inherently the most optimal. When built with haste, infrastructure
can cause unintended harm that lingers for generations. Regulation should not be feared by the
individual, as it exists for their protection. Regulation should not be feared by the company, as it
does not halt progress; rather, it encourages creative work-around solutions. Regulation inspires
innovation. True innovation is born from constraints, and by setting higher standards for
community care, we push the industry toward a more sophisticated, resilient future.



The “Keeler Garden” Green Wall

The concept of a "green wall" or vegetative buffer—inspired by this semester’s partnership with
the Chicago-based organization Keeler Gardens—transforms a data center’s exterior into a
multifunctional environmental asset. This works by mandating a minimum "Green Coverage"
ratio (or some similar metric) in local zoning codes.
By implementing a dense strip of native trees and shrubs (16-32 feet thick), the facility gains a
natural filtration system for nitrogen oxides and particulate matter emitted during generator
testing—the exact pollutants documented as a primary stressor in the Memphis xAI case study..
This living barrier also serves to muffle high-frequency mechanical noise and mitigate the
"Urban Heat Island" effect (United States Environmental Protection Agency), lowering the
ambient temperature for both the residents and the facility itself.
   ●​ Community Win: Improved local aesthetics, buffered air quality, and the restoration of
      local biodiversity.
   ●​ Company Win: Lowered cooling costs due to reduced ambient heat and the security of a
      "social license", per say, to operate without constant PR crises.
Ultimately, this initiative builds a bridge of trust between the industry and its neighbors. As a
compromise, in areas where space is too tight for a full 32-foot buffer, companies can implement
vertical "living facades" or provide direct funding to local organizations to develop off-site green
spaces in the immediate neighborhood. (Note: Keeler Gardens can serve as a placeholder for any
professional landscaping or state-funded ecological project.)



Operational Accountability During Emergency Situations

Operational accountability ensures that when local resources or the power grid are under
pressure, the "invisible giant" is an active participant in the community’s stability, rather than a
drain on it.
A water budgeting and drought-proof operations plan would require facilities to operate on a
strict water budget, incorporating real-time leak detection and prioritizing reclaimed water for

                                                                                                       29
cooling. It would work by requiring developers to submit a "Water Impact Plan" as part of the
zoning process. As a compromise, if reclaimed water infrastructure is not immediately available,
the company must commit to a phased retrofit plan as municipal infrastructure matures.
   ●​ Community Win: Eliminates hidden strain on the municipal drinking water supply and
      protects local aquifers.
   ●​ Company Win: Reduces long-term water supply risks and prevents emergency
      operational restrictions during droughts/water scarcity scenarios.
Moving away from a "diesel-by-default" model, we propose that batteries handle all routine
testing and short-term outages. Policies would cap annual generator testing hours and require
public reporting of backup power events. Generators would be strictly reserved as a "true last
resort" for catastrophic grid failures.
   ●​ Community Win: Significantly less diesel exhaust, reduced noise pollution from testing,
      and lower particulate emissions in residential corridors.
   ●​ Company Win: Improves overall system reliability, reduces fuel logistics/storage risks,
      and lowers wear and tear on backup generators.



Building on SB 4016: Ending “Cross-Subsidy”

To ensure that technological progress does not lead to economic injustice, we propose
implementing structural protections that prevent residents from subsidizing industrial growth.

Transmission security agreements would require "letters of credit" from developers before
breaking ground on massive grid upgrades. This ensures that if a "phantom" project is canceled
mid-build, the developer—not the local ratepayers—is responsible for the multimillion-dollar
substation already under construction.

Community benefit agreements could be triggered for any facility exceeding a specific Megawatt
(MW) threshold. Fees would be indexed to a "dollar-per-MW per year" formula, ensuring the
community’s benefit scales with the facility's power consumption.

   ●​ Community Win: Direct funding for home weatherization, industrial-grade air filters for
      nearby schools, and local bill relief programs.
   ●​ Company Win: Secures the "social license" to operate and reduces the risk of costly
      litigation or PR crises.

Enforcing zoning to data centers where they’d “fit” is a strategy that uses data to pre-designate
industrial brownfields and reuse sites as "preferred" for data center development. Minimum
distance buffers from residential edges would be mandated unless extra, high-tier mitigation (like
the Green Wall) is fully funded.


                                                                                                 30
   ●​ Community Win: Prevents the "surprise" factor of industrial giants appearing next to
      homes, schools, or parks.
   ●​ Company Win: Provides a predictable roadmap with less public backlash and fewer
      expensive site redesigns.

Transparent reporting, but with a “safe harbor” (for legitimate security needs) would require
data, but it must be balanced with operational safety. Companies would be required to publish
aggregated monthly resource data (energy, water, generator runtime) to keep the community
informed. This provision would ensure that specific tenant data or sensitive security details
remain confidential while the impacts remain public.
   ●​ Community win: real data on energy, water, and generator runtime.
   ●​ Company win: avoids oversharing sensitive internal details while still meeting
      accountability.

Infrastructure funded by developers (i.e. grid upgrades) should serve the surrounding
neighborhood as well. When a developer funds a grid upgrade, a portion of that work (such as
feeder hardening) must directly improve local residential reliability. Incentives could be provided
if these upgrades specifically integrate clean energy sources.

   ●​ Community win: reliability improvements and hard protections against bill spikes.
   ●​ Company win: guaranteed interconnection timeline and clearer cost responsibilities.



Limitations

Despite the promise of these solutions, several hurdles remain. Legacy facilities are much harder
to retrofit than "greenfield" projects; while a new building in DeKalb can easily integrate a
32-foot vegetative buffer, a 20-year-old facility in Chicago’s South Loop faces significant spatial
constraints. This means older neighborhoods may continue to experience a higher "annoyance
factor" than areas with newer developments.

Furthermore, there is a significant enforcement asymmetry. Without the independent monitoring
proposed in SB 4016, residents are still forced to rely on company-provided data, which
historically lacks the granularity needed for true accountability. Finally, the speed of the AI race
often outpaces the growth of nature-based solutions—a tree buffer takes years to reach its full
filtration potential, while a data center can be operational in mere months. Recognizing these
gaps is essential to ensuring our recommendations remain grounded in reality, serving as a
framework for persistent advocacy rather than a one-time fix.




                                                                                                   31
Conclusion: Protecting the Right to be Annoyed
Throughout this assessment, we have tracked the footprint of an "invisible giant." We have seen
how AI infrastructure occupies a massive physical reality in our neighborhoods, consuming our
water, straining our power grid, and altering the very air we breathe. From the cautionary tale of
Memphis to the active negotiations in Illinois, the pattern is clear: when AI expansion is driven
by haste rather than harmony, the community is the one expected to shrink.
The "annoyance threshold" is a boundary of human dignity. By making the “invisible giant”
visible through data, mapping, and legislation like SB 4016, we shift the burden of proof. It is no
longer the resident’s job to "learn to be bothered less"—it is the industry’s job to prove it can
coexist without causing harm.
We must protect the "right to be annoyed". Annoyance is our internal alarm system—a signal
that a boundary has been crossed and a social contract has been broken. By reclaiming this right,
we ensure that the progress of artificial intelligence does not require the sacrifice of local
stability.
The giant may be massive, but it is no longer invisible. It is our right to decide how, and where, it
fits into our lives.
